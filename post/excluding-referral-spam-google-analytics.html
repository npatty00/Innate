Excluding Referral Spam in Google Analytics

IMAGE CAPTION

An impressive amount of spam has been appearing in many marketer’s Google analytics profile. Of our web properties, over 80% were affected (link). Luckily there is a simple and quick fix. While the spammers will continue to innovate and get around filters, learning how to identify and exclude this type of traffic will beat them to the punch. Following this guide will not only teach you how to get rid of current referral spam, but identify and block any future spam as soon as you see it.

The current spam campaign affecting our websites looks like this: (Not so tough to spot, is it?)

To stop this bot traffic from being recorded in the future, we’ll create a filter to exclude this specific type of traffic. When you see other types of spam, you can follow the same steps to create new filters.

On the top navigation bar of Google analytics, click the “Admin” tab. Navigate to the furthest left column, “Account”. Click “All Filters”.

Click the red “+ ADD FILTER” button. Use the following settings:

Name your filter so anyone can understand what it does. This filter will exclude any traffic whose “language” attribute contains the Cyrillic letter that looks like “G”. (More on why we did this further down) Choose the filter type of Custom. Predefined filters won’t accomplish what we need. Choose Exclude. This option will find any traffic you identify, and stop it from appearing your data. In the Filter Field, search for Language Settings. In Filer Pattern, copy and paste the Cyrillic “G”.

When a user visits your website their web browser sends a little extra data that is stored in the header. This includes information like what kind of device they use, the size of the screen, and the language to display the site in. Our Filter Field says to look at what language is provided in the header of the traffic to the site. The Filter Pattern says to only grab traffic whose language contains* the fake letter “G”.

Now you can add your filter to a view. We highly recommend creating a test view and only applying this filter to that view. After a couple days, go back and check if it's working as intended. If all your data looks okay, then you can apply this filter to your primary view. For future spammers, simply identify any dimension (you can see a list in the dropdown of Filter Field) and a character or set of characters that will uniquely identify that spam. For example, for spam traffic coming in as referral from socialbuttons.xyz, create a filter with the Filter Field of “Referral” and the Filter Pattern of “socialbuttons.yxz”

*This actually uses regular expression.
